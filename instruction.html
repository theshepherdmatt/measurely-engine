How to run a sweep in REW

A step-by-step guide to capturing the perfect measurement.

What are we actually measuring?

Measurely isn't testing the quality of your speakers. It measures the acoustic response of your room based on exactly where your speakers and listening chair are placed.

You don't just hear the direct sound from the speakers—you hear a complex mix of energy bouncing off the walls, floor, ceiling, and building up in corners. This is why the microphone must point straight up and why we sweep both speakers together: to capture the total acoustic signature of the room precisely as your ears experience it in that specific spot.

1 Physical Setup

Place your measurement microphone (like a UMIK-1) exactly at your main listening position—right where the centre of your head usually is.

Crucial: Ensure the microphone capsule is pointing dead straight up towards the ceiling, not forwards at the speakers.

Measuring with the mic pointed upwards captures the room's energy (reflections from the walls, floor, and ceiling) accurately, which is exactly what Measurely needs to diagnose the space.

2 REW Configuration

Open Room EQ Wizard (REW) and click the Measure button in the top left corner.

Set your sweep parameters as follows:

  • Type: Sine sweep
  • Range: 20 Hz to 20,000 Hz
  • Length: 256k or 512k

Mono Measurement: You must measure both the Left and Right speakers at the exact same time. In the Output dropdown, select L+R or ensure your audio interface routes the sweep to both channels simultaneously. Measurely analyzes how the speakers interact with the room together.

3 Run the Sweep

Click Start Measuring. Step away from the microphone and remain perfectly quiet while the sweep runs. Any background noise (doors closing, footsteps, breathing too close to the mic) can pollute the measurement.

4 Exporting the WAV File

Once REW has captured the sweep and generated the response graph on your screen, you need to export the raw impulse response for Measurely to analyze.

  1. Go to the top menu and click File
  2. Navigate to Export → Export impulse response as WAV...
  3. Make sure the format is set to 32-bit float (or 24-bit) and configured to Mono.
  4. Save the file somewhere convenient on your computer.
